 Residential land values

Residential overview

Overall, residential land values held steady in the Port Stephens local government area between July 2023 and July 2024. This, however, was not consistent in all areas.

Low density residential properties in Wallalong & Seaham experienced a moderate decrease. Demand in these semi-rural areas softened due to locational factors and high construction costs.

In contrast, low density residential zoned properties in Raymond Terrace south and Heatherbrae along with medium density zoned properties in Raymond Terrace experienced moderate increases due to increased amenities in Raymond Terrace to accommodate population growth. General residential zoned development sites in Raymond Terrace north and Kings Hill experienced a moderate increase due to their progress in development to meet a strong demand and future drive for more housing.

By contrast, low density residential properties in Boat Harbour experienced a strong increase. Ocean front properties in Boat Harbour, Anna Bay and Fisherman’s Bay experienced moderate increases. Strong demand from both home buyers and investors was seen in these seaside areas which offer a relaxed coastal lifestyle in close proximity to the main, northern tourism area of Port Stephens including Nelson Bay.

Medium density residential zoned properties in Salamander Bay and Soldiers Point experienced a strong decrease associated with high construction costs. Similarly, low density waterfront properties in Salamander Bay, Soldiers Point and Corlette experienced a moderate decrease where demand for high end prices softened. These locations are an extension of the main tourism area of Port Stephens and are within close proximity to the coast and local shops.

Demand for residential properties in Port Stephens is supported by its commuting distance from Newcastle.

 Rural land values

Rural overview

Overall, there was a slight increase in land values in the Port Stephens local government area between July 2023 and July 2024.

This trend was broadly consistent across most locations, however rural properties above 40 hectares experienced strong increase due to coming off a low value base and the relative affordability within proximity to Newcastle.

Lifestyle rural properties east of the Pacific Highway between 5 and 40 ha experienced a slight decrease due to a downturn in market sentiment for this property type which tends to be environmentally constrained.

 Added value of improvement tables

The added value of improvement tables show dollar values per square metre for the added value of improvements for the main residential property types in a local government area. It is the value the improvements add to the value of the land as demonstrated by the analysis of property sales.


Read more

Land value is the value of your land only. It does not include the value of your home or other structures. The term added value of improvements is the dollar value that your home or other structures add to land value.

The added value of improvements is their worth as demonstrated by the property market in a local government area and is generally not equal to the replacement or insurance value.

The added values of improvements are determined by the analysis of a broad range of market sales including vacant land and properties with improvements in a range of conditions, ages, sizes and styles. The sale prices of the range of properties is then compared. Where the land is comparable the difference in sale price is the added value of the improvements.

This analysis is then included in a table identifying different building types on a dollar rate per square metre basis. Those rates are then used to deduct the added value of the improvements from other sales in the area to determine their land values.

More information on this "paired sales analysis" approach is contained in the Valuer General's policy on the 'Valuation of Single Residential Land' available on our website at valuergeneral.nsw.gov.au.
