General overview

Land values across Bega Valley Local Government area between 2023 and 2024 remained steady overall. This trend was consistent across residential and rural market segments, while commercial properties showed a slight increase and industrial properties showed a strong increase.

While there remains a steady demand by owner occupiers and investors looking to coastal locations for retirement or investment, continuing interest rate rises, rising inflation and increasing construction costs have resulted in a steadying or decrease across most market sectors.

 Residential land values

Residential overview

Residential land values overall have remained steady across the Bega Valley local government area, but has varied between locations.

Low density residential in the village of Boydtown, and environmental properties around Bega and within Mogareeka have shown moderate to strong decreases. These decreases are a result of the longer-term impact of the 2019 - 2020 bushfires in impacted areas, increasing costs to build on flood liable land, relatively high cost to purchase, and higher cost of insurance to cover such properties.

Large lot residential properties within the northern villages of Bega LGA, the Northern Village of Cobargo, and Low density residential in Bermagui have shown moderate increases. The southern Villages of Candelo and Wolumla have shown strong increases. Relatively restricted supply and sustained demand from purchasers relocating from more densely populated areas have supported value movements in these localities.

Medium Density zoned properties in Bermagui, Bega and Pambula have experienced moderate to strong increases. These increases align with mixed use properties in Bega and Pambula, which showed strong to very strong increases. Demand remains strong for properties with greater and more flexible development potential.

Proximity to Canberra and major southern NSW regional centres, improved transport routes, and affordability for retirees and holiday home seekers underpin values in the LGA. However, higher interest rates, higher inflation and increased construction costs have steadied values across residential zones.

 Rural land values

Rural overview

Values in most of the rural areas have remained steady from July 2023 through to July 2024 with relatively steady sales volumes throughout the period. This movement is not consistent and varies between locations.

Rural properties generally under 5 hectares within the northern and central portions of the LGA have shown moderate increases, with demand from purchasers relocating from more densely populated areas supporting value movements in these localities.

Genuine rural grazing land and large lot rural residential holdings remain highly sought after along the coastal strip. A relatively limited supply coupled with the tightly held nature of the market has under pinned steady market growth in this sector.

 Added value of improvement tables

The added value of improvement tables show dollar values per square metre for the added value of improvements for the main residential property types in a local government area. It is the value the improvements add to the value of the land as demonstrated by the analysis of property sales.


Read more

Land value is the value of your land only. It does not include the value of your home or other structures. The term added value of improvements is the dollar value that your home or other structures add to land value.

The added value of improvements is their worth as demonstrated by the property market in a local government area and is generally not equal to the replacement or insurance value.

The added values of improvements are determined by the analysis of a broad range of market sales including vacant land and properties with improvements in a range of conditions, ages, sizes and styles. The sale prices of the range of properties is then compared. Where the land is comparable the difference in sale price is the added value of the improvements.

This analysis is then included in a table identifying different building types on a dollar rate per square metre basis. Those rates are then used to deduct the added value of the improvements from other sales in the area to determine their land values.

More information on this "paired sales analysis" approach is contained in the Valuer General's policy on the 'Valuation of Single Residential Land' available on our website at valuergeneral.nsw.gov.au.
