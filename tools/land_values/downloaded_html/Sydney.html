Sydney City region

The information in this interactive summary was prepared by Valuer General NSW. 2024 land values in the City of Sydney area were prepared by Quotable Value Aust. P/L.

General overview

Between July 2023 and July 2024, land values across the City of Sydney local government area showed a moderate overall increase. However, this trend varied across different market segments, with industrial land remaining steady. The commercial sector experienced a moderate increase in land values overall. However, commercial zoned land located in the CBD is still impacted by limited tenant demand and although the city vacancy rate has stabilised in the last 6 months, it is still above the long-term average. Occupiers are still taking the opportunity to make the “flight to quality”, leaving older buildings waiting to be repurposed. High interest rates and rising construction costs have constrained new development, while the low number of sales in the CBD provides evidence of a stable market in many areas. In the residential sector, values have also shown a moderate increase. The limited supply of industrial properties within the local government area has remained stable, resulting in an uneven impact across the various sectors.

 Residential land values

Residential overview

The overall trend for the residential values in the City of Sydney was a moderate increase between July 2023 and July 2024. This is driven by strong demand and limited rental availability in the inner suburbs. However, this upward trend is not uniform across all zones and suburbs. Gardeners Road in Rosebery showed a slight decrease and non-terraced residential zoned properties in Pyrmont remained steady during the same period.

Overall, mixed-use zones across the council area have generally shown moderate increases. In contrast, mainly commercial properties with mixed-use zoning in Blackwattle Bay and Newtown remained steady, and there was a slight decrease in part of Surry Hills south of Foveaux Street.

Unzoned land in the Eastern Harbour City Walsh Bay Precinct has remained steady. The area includes a number of high value strata buildings where there have been fewer sales this year. The commercial precinct within this area also remained steady.

 Commercial land values

Commercial overview

The overall trend for the commercial values in the City of Sydney local government area was a moderate increase between July 2023 and July 2024. However, commercial zoned land located in the CBD is still impacted by limited tenant demand and although the city vacancy rate has stabilised in the last 6 months, it is still above the long term average. Occupiers are still taking the opportunity to make the “flight to quality”, leaving older buildings waiting to be repurposed. High interest rates and rising construction costs have constrained new development, while the low number of sales in the CBD provides evidence of a stable market in many areas. There have been a low number of large sales in the CBD, particularly in the last 6 months, and the land values within the CBD have generally shown a steady to slight increase. Notably, there have been stronger sales on heritage properties and small to medium-sized properties in certain areas of the city, which have supported an increase in some values. Prime retail spaces within the city remained steady, including those in the CBD, Chinatown and the Western Corridor. Similarly, retail properties in stratum developments across the CBD remained steady overall.

The local centre zones throughout the City of Sydney local government area have exhibited inconsistent trends across various suburbs. The Victoria Park local centre and the Glebe local centre (excluding Glebe Point Road) experienced very strong increases. Conversely, the local centres in Darlinghurst and Millers Point showed moderate decreases, while Potts Point and Ultimo showed slight decreases and Pyrmont's local centre showed a strong decrease. Newtown showed a combination of slight and moderate decreases and Camperdown's local centre remained steady between July 2023 to July 2024. In general, properties in local centre zones tended to follow the broader trends seen in nearby residential zones.

In the productivity support zones, the overall trend was a moderate increase in land values. The productivity support zoned South Sydney properties located at the south end of Botany Road exhibited a very strong increase. These properties, which include bulk retail, car showrooms, small industrial spaces, and business estates, benefit from their close proximity to key infrastructure. Botany Road provides easy access to major locations such as Port Botany and Sydney Airport. Additionally, this area is continuing to undergo significant urban renewal.

 Industrial land values

Industrial overview

Industrial land in the local government area has remained steady between July 2023 and July 2024. While the overall supply of industrial zoned property in South Sydney has declined, demand has stabilised. This demand is primarily driven by the new industries of logistics, e-commerce, and data centres. Key infrastructure improvements, such as the St Peters Interchange and the extension of the M4 motorway, have also contributed to enhancing connectivity and accessibility for businesses.

 Added value of improvement tables

The added value of improvement tables show dollar values per square metre for the added value of improvements for the main residential property types in a local government area. It is the value the improvements add to the value of the land as demonstrated by the analysis of property sales.


Read more

Land value is the value of your land only. It does not include the value of your home or other structures. The term added value of improvements is the dollar value that your home or other structures add to land value.

The added value of improvements is their worth as demonstrated by the property market in a local government area and is generally not equal to the replacement or insurance value.

The added values of improvements are determined by the analysis of a broad range of market sales including vacant land and properties with improvements in a range of conditions, ages, sizes and styles. The sale prices of the range of properties is then compared. Where the land is comparable the difference in sale price is the added value of the improvements.

This analysis is then included in a table identifying different building types on a dollar rate per square metre basis. Those rates are then used to deduct the added value of the improvements from other sales in the area to determine their land values.

More information on this "paired sales analysis" approach is contained in the Valuer General's policy on the 'Valuation of Single Residential Land' available on our website at valuergeneral.nsw.gov.au.
